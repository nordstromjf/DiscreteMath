Definition 10.1.2.

A graph, \(G\text{,}\) consists of a finite set, \(V(G)\text{,}\) of vertices and a finite set, \(E(G)\text{,}\) of edges. Each edge is assocated with two vertices (it may be the same vertex) called endpoints. In the above example, \(V(G)=\{v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4\}, E(G)=\{e_1, e_2, e_3, e_4, e_5\}\text{.}\) The endpoints of edge \(e_1\) are \(v_1, v_2\text{.}\)

If two edges have the same endpoints, we say they are parallel.

A loop is an edge where both endpints are the same vertex.

Two vertices connected by an edge are called adjacent. For example, \(v_2\) is adjacent to \(v_3\text{,}\) but \(v_3\) is not adjacent to \(v_4\text{.}\)

A vertex of a loop is adjacent to itself.

An edge is incident on each of its endpoints.

Two edges that are incident on the same vertex are adjacent. For example, \(e_1\) and \(e_2\) are adjacent, but \(e_2\) and \(e_5\) are not adjacent.

An isolated vertex is one with no incident edges.

A graph with no vertices is the empty graph.
